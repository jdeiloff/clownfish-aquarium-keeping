Tank Mates for the Clownfish in Home Aquariums

How they live in the wild

Clownfishes normally lives in small groups with a very defined dominance hierarchy, where the largest female is the leader of that reef with its only breeding male partner, so other juveniles live in lower social levels. They have to find their own anemonaes to form their own breeding couple. They are found living among many other fishes in the wild, although they tend to fight with similar fishes like Damsels and other species of clownfishes. They mostly live hidden in the anemonaes arms or in rock caves, they only swim in the open to look for food. Algaes, shrimps, corals, and a wide variety of animals live in the reefs with the clownfish. They do not swim large distances and tend to live in very shallow waters.

Suitable aquarium mates

They can live in pairs of the same species, or small groups if the tank is very large and they can have their own territories. They tend to fight with other clownfish species, as well as Damsel fishes. Wrasses, Dartfish, butterflyfish, Cardinalfish are suitable partners. Bottom dwellers such as Blennies and Gobies are very territorial but as they live at a different level of the tank, they are compatible. Bigger and very active fishes like Angelifish or Tangs (like Dory) should be monitored to make sure they are not causing stress. Aggresive fishes such as Groupers, Lionfish, eels and Trigger fish should not be kept together as they will try to eat the clownfishes. Active invertebrates such as Peppermint Shrimps and Harlequin Shrimps or most of the reef aquaria snails, can be easily kept with clownfishes. Most of the soft and hard corals are compatible (given the right water and artificial light conditions) and are a great addition to the home aquarium reef. Anemonaes are a bit hard to keep and their symbiotic pairing is not guaranteed (usually there are no predators in the tank so why bother). But if achieved, it is a beautiful scenary to witness and show off. Some suitable anemonaes are the Bubble Tip, Magneficent and Leathery Sea Anemonaes.
